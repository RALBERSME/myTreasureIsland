Example of feelings:


Let's take this case from everyday life. You are on your way to a birthday party for which you have spent hours baking a cake. Now you just have to cross the street with the cake in your hand when a bicycle courier hits you without braking on the zebra crossing. You fall over and the cake flies in a high arc onto the street so that the next car drives over it.

Shame: "Oh dear. This is going to be embarrassing at the party. Everyone has been waiting for the cake and I'm not even able to look after it on the street and bring it to the party in one piece. I'm so embarrassed. I'm no good either."

Guilt:"It was obvious that this would happen to me. How incapable I am. I can't even carry a cake across the street. I really should have looked to the left again. I just walk through life too dreamily."

Apathy:"Whatever, that sort of thing happens all the time. They probably wouldn't have liked my cake anyway. They won't miss the cake or me. I should just stop going to the party. Life is really shit."

Sadness"Oh my goodness. The cake is just mush. What will the birthday boy think? I'll massively disappoint him and then our friendship won't be as harmonious as it was before. I could cry."

Fear"The front wheel of the bike is completely dented. Now he's sure to freak out because he needs the bike to earn money. It's better if I don't say anything, don't complain and just keep walking. It's best if I don't ask him to replace the cake either. The guy is much younger and bigger than me. He'll yell at me or give me another Slap him in the face."

Demand:"Just wait, you kid, you're going to pay me dearly for that. First I'm going to demand a master cake from the bakery around the corner, then he should buy me a new outfit, because I've been lying in the dirt because of him. And after the party I'll ask my lawyer what else we can get out of it."

Anger:"He's lost his mind! Can't the idiot pay attention? I'll kick him in his damn bike. He'll be lucky if I don't kill him. Guys like that should be banned from riding bikes. "

Pride:"Hey, you idiot, look where you're going. That's probably why you have to deliver pizza, because otherwise you can't get anything done. You're probably such a big loser that you don't even have insurance. Bad luck, then it's going to be really expensive for you. I, on the other hand, am insured with the most expensive and best insurance company, and they'll give you a hard time."

Courage:"Well, it was a bad turn. But we're both insured. The insurance companies will sort it out. I'll make sure that we settle it out of court and in such a way that you don't get into big trouble with your boss."

Neutrality: "Well, that's life. It can happen to anyone."

Willingness:"My God, the bike courier is beside himself. He is worried about his job. What can I do to calm him down? I tell him that our insurance will sort it out and then we will be fine."

Acceptance:"Phew, we were lucky. It could have been worse. Basically, only the cake is ruined, I am a bit dusty and his front wheel is dented. All of that can be replaced for little money. Basically, nothing serious has happened."

Reason:"How can we get this thing off the ice as quickly as possible? I'll google it. Look, there is a bike repair shop just around the corner. You can have the front wheel replaced there. I quickly shake the dust off myself and get a frozen cake from the supermarket. Then the party can still go on."

Love:"I hope nothing has happened to you other than the bike. Calm down, we'll let our insurance take care of it all. It's really not a big deal. I've also caused an accident on my bike before and I can understand how you feel now. Don't worry. (puts arm on shoulder)"

Primary and secondary feelings:



There are primary and secondary feelings. Primary feelings are those that underlie our actions. Our primary feelings are not always immediately apparent to ourselves or others, as they can be covered up by secondary feelings.

The following example should illustrate this:

Till is violently bumped into by someone. The primary feeling is anger. Till's impulse would be to complain. But he has learned that anger is dangerous because then the other person could also become angry and threaten him (fear). The other person could make a loud scene so that passers-by would notice (shame), and perhaps Till himself was not paying attention in the end (guilt). All of these secondary emotions regulate Till's original anger, so that in the end he just lets the matter rest with the friendly words "everything's OK, nothing happened". Later, Till gets angry with himself and criticizes himself for not defending himself, for being anxious and lacking self-confidence, and he sadly withdraws.

Primary emotions are natural and serve a purpose. They last until the situation is over. Secondary emotions, on the other hand, are constructed; they serve to regulate relationships and our inner life and can last much longer. Therefore, it is important to work out whether the emotion we are currently feeling is a primary or a secondary one.

Stress:



Stress is on everyone's lips these days. Stress is understood as the natural reaction of the body and soul to external or internal stresses and challenges. This applies to both difficult situations, but also to situations that we see as positive, such as bungee jumping or a corporate career.

The stress triggers cause the body to release more hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol. This increases our reaction ability. The purpose of these physical reactions is to ensure survival in dangerous situations by enabling us to perform at our best. Stress therefore has a motivating and activating effect, so that we can better master challenges.

If the stress load is too high or constant, there is a risk that the positive effect will turn negative. Constant tension and excessive demands can make you mentally and physically ill. This also applies to too much positive stress.

The greatest stress is not caused by external stimuli (noise, environment) but by the pressure of our own suppressed emotions. This is precisely why it is important to deal with your feelings in more depth.

The human alarm system is structured as follows. First, the alarm reaction comes from the cerebral cortex. From there it reaches deeper layers of the brain such as the hypthalamus, then into the adrenal glands and the bloodstream (cortisol and adrenaline). Brain hormones are also released and the sympathetic nervous system is stimulated. The body is now flooded with adrenaline and prepares for fight or flight. The person thus finds himself in a struggle for survival.

You may have noticed that your body goes crazy at times when you want to relax. You go on holiday stressed and then get sick or depressed. This is partly because your body has become accustomed to high cortisol levels and is addicted to maintaining them.

In the resistance phase, the phase that follows the alarm reaction, the body tries to restore a homeostatic balance. This is where hormonal changes and shifts in metabolism occur. This can lead to water retention in the body (e.g. in the legs and feet).

This is followed by the exhaustion phase. This is adrenaline exhaustion, which causes the immune system to become weak. It can no longer compensate for the effects of stress. The suppression of the immune system and the depletion of energy reserves lead to illness and, in extreme cases, death.

Permanent stress also affects the digestive system. This leads to hyperacidity and an overproduction of digestive enzymes can lead to the formation of ulcers. The cardiovascular system in particular suffers. Stress is one of the main causes of high blood pressure, heart attacks and heart attacks.

Fury:


When we get angry, it is a reaction to a rule that we have internalized being broken. Often we are not even aware of this rule. These rules often come from our childhood, when we adopted certain ideas about how something should or should not be through education or imitation of our environment. The internalization of these rules therefore took place at a time when we were not yet adults and therefore usually without questioning the beliefs we had adopted. Such beliefs can be, for example, that as a boy you are not allowed to cry or that it is not fair if you are not allowed to play, that friends always have to stick by you, etc.

When we are angry, we want to use all means possible to ensure that "our" rules are followed. To this end, we may manipulate, force others to do something, shout, threaten, use our authority, use violence (psychological or physical, against things or people) and much more. This can cause the situation to escalate even further.

It is therefore important to recognize that the core message of anger is our belief that something has to change. One of our rules has been broken and we are therefore pushing for this breach to be healed.

It is very important to first recognize which rule is behind our anger, which we see as broken. The next step is to accept that this is our rule, which the other person does not even have to know. The question also arises as to whether our rule is correct at all. It is also important to question whether our rule is correct.

The following attitudes are less helpful: negative assessments, condemnations, inability to forgive.

Instead, the interest of wanting to understand one another should come to the fore. What works best is to ask simple, open questions to find out why the other person behaves the way they do.

It is crucial that we recognize that our anger is never wrong in itself. It only indicates that we have been triggered by something and that an expectation/rule of ours has been violated. We also tend to believe what others tell us when we are angry. "You are acting like a child. You are an idiot." This can make us even angrier. This realization is also important if someone else gets angry because of our behavior. So it would be appropriate to convey to the other person that it is not they as a person who is stupid or childish, but only their behavior. Since words can cause more damage in the long run than physical violence, we should choose them carefully.

Instead of harming others, anger should be used productively.

Don't treat others in a way you don't want them to treat you. It's best to pause for a moment to calm down before you say anything. In some situations, it may be appropriate to leave the place for a while to create a break. Then think about what your long-term priorities are. Do you want to be right in this argument, or is the relationship more important to you?
How can the resulting communication gap be bridged? Now it's time to learn to understand the other person's position, if necessary, to define what everyone has understood and how, and to convey how you want to be understood yourself. It is also helpful to capture the emotions in pictures.

Ultimately, the decisive factor is that we let the anger go, because it blocks our strength, our freedom and our development. The anger does not come from the other person, it is in us and therefore our issue. But if we do not let it go, we will be the ones who continue to suffer from it.

Ways to let go:

Mentally go back through your life; does this anger sound familiar to you? When did it first occur to you? What was the situation like back then? What triggered this emotion?

What was wrong back then? What did you subjectively perceive as wrong back then?

What bad insight can you learn from it? What good insight can you learn from it?

Talk in your mind to the perpetrator who treated you wrongly and leave the responsibility for it with him.

Now say goodbye to this old situation forever and take the good insights/learning effects that you gained from it with you into your life today.

Apologize to the person you got angry at today. Show the person that you value them and that your relationship with them is important to you. Express your regret and ask for forgiveness.

If your rule that was broken was not good, change your rule. Ask yourself whether the rule really corresponds to how you think about yourself and the world today.

Future pace: Imagine yourself in a situation in the future in which you are already applying the new, revised rule. What will it be like? How will you feel then?

Conclusion: Anger feeds anger.

Even purely factual arguments can lead to the escalation continuing. Therefore, perceive the emotion and respond to the emotion. If that is not possible at the moment, take a break. Intense emotions, such as anger, are an expression of a lack of empathy. Empathy is the ability to understand how the other person feels and why they feel the way they do. Try to see the situation from their perspective.

Recognize that the conflict is not just about you, but about everyone.

How to deal with angry people:

If someone gets angry, then one of their rules has been broken. If they are constantly angry, then they constantly have the feeling that their rules are being broken. These rules can be reasonable or unreasonable and they can come from a time when they had a completely different meaning.

Be part of the solution to the problem, not the reinforcement of the problem.

Show empathy, because empathy means understanding. Empathy does not mean that you agree with everything or accept the other person's rule or behavior.
